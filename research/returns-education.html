Abstract

This thesis estimates the causal return to schooling using Australian microdata. Following the proximity to college identification from Card (1993), I use distance to the nearest university at age 17 as an instrument for years of education. The empirical strategy applies two stage least squares to obtain local average treatment effects, with extensive validity checks on instrument strength, monotonicity, and exclusion restrictions. Results demonstrate economically meaningful and statistically significant returns to education that are robust across cohorts, specifications, and sampling windows in the Australian context.

Methods

  • Design: Instrumental Variables with Two Stage Least Squares
  • Instrument: Distance to nearest university at age 17 (proximity to college)
  • Identification: Relevance and exogeneity tested via first stage F statistics, over identification where applicable, and cohort specific robustness
  • Controls: Birth cohort, state fixed effects, parental background proxies, urbanity, and local labour market conditions
  • Estimands: LATE for compliers, compared with OLS for bias diagnostics

Data

Australian microdata with individual earnings, schooling, demographics, and location

Geographic linkage to the nearest university network and historical openings

Sample restricted to prime age workers with complete earnings and education records

Results (Summary)

  • First stage: Strong instrument, first stage F typically above 20
  • 2SLS: One additional year of schooling increases earnings by 7‚Äì10 percent depending on cohort and specification
  • Robustness: Stable to alternative distance measures, sample trims, and additional controls
  • Policy Implication: Access to nearby universities raises educational attainment and yields sizeable wage gains
