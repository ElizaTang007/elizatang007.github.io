Returns to Education — A Causal Analysis Using Instrumental Variables (Methodological Review)

Status: Completed (High Distinction)
Course: ECMT3997 Interdisciplinary Impact in Econometrics, University of Sydney
Credit: 6 CP · Focus: Causal Inference for Real-World Policy Problems

Abstract

This study conducts a methodological review and replication of Card (1993)'s instrumental-variables framework for estimating the causal returns to schooling. Following Card's original identification strategy, the instrument is a binary indicator for whether an individual lived near a four-year college (nearc4) during adolescence. Using a two-stage least squares (2SLS) design, the analysis compares ordinary least squares (OLS) and IV estimates, assesses instrument validity, and discusses the implications for local average treatment effects (LATE). The replication finds an OLS estimate of 7.47%, an IV estimate of 13.15%, and a strong first stage (F ≈ 182). The findings suggest that OLS underestimates the true causal return to education, though external validity and exclusion-restriction assumptions remain important caveats.

Methodology

Design: Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS) Instrumental Variables

Instrument: nearc4 — whether an individual lived near a four-year college at age 17; interaction with parental education used as an alternative instrument

Identification and Validity Checks:

  • Relevance: First-stage F-statistic ≈ 182, well above the conventional threshold of 10
  • Exclusion Restriction: Evaluated through reduced-form regressions and augmented controls; potential threats include residential sorting and local externalities
  • Monotonicity: Reasonable under the assumption that college proximity lowers educational costs
  • Weak Instrument Concern: Addressed using Staiger–Stock rule-of-thumb and over-identification tests when applicable

Estimators:

  • LATE (Local Average Treatment Effect) as the causal target of the 2SLS estimator
  • OLS as a benchmark for bias diagnosis
  • Controls: Gender, race, region, urbanization, experience (and its square), and proxies for family background

Data

Context: Conceptual replication of Card (1993) using the U.S. NLS Young Men data structure, not Australian microdata

Variables: Log wage, years of schooling, nearc4 indicator, demographic and regional characteristics

Results (Summary)

  • First Stage: Strong instrument (F ≈ 182)
  • Reduced Form: nearc4 coefficient ≈ 0.0421, significant but modest in magnitude
  • 2SLS Estimate: Return to an additional year of education ≈ 13.15%
  • OLS Estimate: ≈ 7.47%
  • Alternative Instrument: nearc4 × parental education yields ≈ 10.06%, confirming robustness
  • Interpretation: The IV estimate represents the LATE for compliers—individuals whose education decisions are affected by college accessibility—rather than the overall average treatment effect (ATE)

Discussion and Policy Implications

External Validity and Exclusion Risk:

Residential sorting and spatial spillovers may weaken the exclusion restriction; local urbanization and educational clustering require additional controls

Methodological Considerations:

With heterogeneous treatment effects, 2SLS estimates are variance-weighted averages, potentially overstating returns for marginal compliers; semi-parametric or machine-learning extensions can improve robustness

Policy Insight:

Enhancing college accessibility can increase educational attainment and earnings potential, but LATE should not be over-extrapolated to the general population
